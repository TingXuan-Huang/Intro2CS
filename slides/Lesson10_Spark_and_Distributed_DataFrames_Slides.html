1 · The cast

One driver, many executors

The driver

The program you talk to — your notebook's spark object. It holds the plan, splits work into tasks, hands tasks out, and combines the partial results.

The driver coordinates; it does not want your whole dataset in its lap.

The executors

The workers that hold the data and do the work — each one processes its own slice, in parallel with the others.

On a real cluster they are separate machines. In local[2], two worker threads on your laptop.

Sound familiar? It is the client/server split from Lesson 6, turned inward: one coordinator that asks, many workers that answer.
Every Spark question — "why lazy?", "why shuffle?" — comes back to this split: the plan lives in one place, the data lives in many.
1 · Partitions

A Spark DataFrame is sliced into partitions

rows 0–29partition 0 · executor A
rows 30–59partition 1 · executor B

What a partition is

A horizontal slice of the rows, living on one executor. The DataFrame you type against is the union of its partitions — no single machine ever needs to hold all of it.

What a task is

One unit of work on one partition. A job over 200 partitions becomes 200 tasks that can run wherever there are free executors.

Partitions are why Spark scales: the same per-slice work runs on 2 slices or 2,000 — Lesson 4's "shape of the work" made physical.

1 · The central distinction

Transformations record; actions execute

KindExamplesWhat happens when you call it
Transformationselect · filter · groupBy · join · withColumna step is recorded onto the plan — no data is touched, it returns instantly
Actionshow · count · write · toPandas · collectthe whole recorded plan finally executes across the partitions

The pandas contrast

A pandas operation usually runs immediately — you type it, it happens. A Spark transformation such as filter merely describes what should happen.

Why the split exists

Deferring execution lets Spark see the whole plan before running any of it — and coordinate the work across partitions in one optimized pass.

Transformations describe what; actions decide when. Nothing runs until an action asks for actual rows.

1 · The action to distrust

collect() pulls everything into the driver's lap

The trap

collect() (and toPandas()) ship every row of the result to the driver. On the retail toy that is 60 rows. On real data it is the single most common way to crash a Spark job — the distributed answer un-distributes itself into one process's memory.

The habit

Keep results distributed until they are small: show() to peek, count() to size, write to store. Reach for toPandas() only when the result is a small summary — like our five country-month rows.

This is why the exercise forbids collect() and toPandas() inside your functions: your job is to return the plan; the checker decides when to execute it.
Avoid collect() unless the result is truly small enough for the driver. Aggregates first, collect last.
1 · Checkpoint

Why bother being lazy?

Pandas runs each line immediately and you have never missed laziness. What does Spark gain by refusing to run filter and select until an action arrives?
Two things. Optimization: at action time Spark sees the whole pipeline, so it can rearrange it — push filters early, read only needed columns, plan joins — exactly as a SQL engine rewrites a query it can see in full (Lessons 8–9). Coordination: the plan, not the data, is what travels to executors; each partition then runs the optimized recipe locally. Eager execution would lock in a naive order and force intermediate results into existence that nobody asked for.
Laziness is declarativeness in disguise: you state what, and the engine — seeing everything — chooses how and when.

2 · The Spark spelling

Join, derive, group, aggregate — one lazy chain

    .withColumn('month', F.date_format('transaction_date', 'yyyy-MM'))

One real difference

The right column never runs when you type it. Everything in PySpark's grammar is a transformation; the timing column is the whole story.

One cosmetic difference

Column references are wrapped: F.col('quantity') instead of df.quantity — because the expression must travel to executors, it is an object, not a value.

If you can write the pandas line, you can write the Spark line. The translation is mechanical; the execution model is the new content.

2 · Checkpoint

Which spelling is faster?

A teammate insists the DataFrame chain must be faster than spark.sql(...) "because SQL is an extra layer of parsing." Are they right?
No — and explain() proves it. Both spellings are converted to the same logical plan and handed to the same optimizer (Spark's Catalyst); the physical plan that executes is identical. The parsing cost is microseconds, once, on the driver. This is the deep lesson of Lessons 8–9 resurfacing: in a declarative system, you choose the spelling; the engine chooses the procedure — so style, readability, and your team's habits are the real deciding factors.
Never argue performance between two spellings of the same plan. Run explain() on both and watch the plans coincide.

3 · The distinction that transfers

groupBy collapses; a window describes

groupBy

Ten customers become one row per country-customer group — you keep the summary, you lose the individuals. That is what "aggregate" means.

dense_rank().over(window)

Every row survives, and each gains a new column computed relative to its group: this customer's standing within its country.

Same window, three engines now: Lesson 9 SQL's DENSE_RANK() OVER (...), pandas' groupby(...).rank(method="dense"), and today's F.dense_rank().over(rank_window). One idea, three spellings.
A window calculates over a group while retaining its rows — the idea is identical to Lesson 9; only the punctuation moved.

4 · Physical layout

Where the rows live starts to matter

Everything so far ignored layout

Units 1–3 treated partitions as an invisible implementation detail — the same code runs however the rows are sliced.

Unit 4 makes layout a choice

Two deliberate decisions: how data is arranged on disk (partitioned Parquet), and how it moves between executors (shuffles). Both change cost, not correctness.

Lesson 2 said "storage is not neutral" about file formats. Unit 4 is the same sentence at a different scale: the arrangement of files decides how much of them a query must read.
Correct first, laid-out second: layout choices never change the answer — only how much work producing it costs.

4 · Shuffles

Some questions force rows to move

Why movement is unavoidable

groupBy('country') must bring every row of a country together to sum it — but those rows are scattered across partitions on different executors. So Spark redistributes them: that redistribution is a shuffle.

Who needs one

groupBy, join, and windows — anything where rows sharing a key must meet. Row-local work (filter, withColumn, select) never shuffles: each row's answer needs no other row.

Our config set spark.sql.shuffle.partitions to 2 — that line in the setup cell is literally "after a shuffle, deal the rows into two piles."
A shuffle is often necessary and always costly at scale: rows serialized, sent across the network, and re-grouped by key.

4 · The cost, honestly

repartition is a deliberate layout change, not a speed button

What a shuffle spends

  • Serialization — every moving row encoded to bytes and back.
  • Network — rows crossing between executors (machines, at scale).
  • Disk — shuffle files written and re-read on both sides.

How to think about it

  • Shuffles are often necessary — a join is worth its shuffle.
  • Filter and prune before the shuffle, so fewer rows move.
  • Call repartition only when you can say which layout you are buying and why.
On our 60-row toy the shuffle is invisible. The mental model you are building is for the day the same groupBy moves 60 billion rows between racks.
Count shuffles the way Lesson 4 counted comparisons: they are the dominant term in a distributed job's cost.
4 · Checkpoint

Which of these moves rows?

Four steps from this lesson's own pipeline: ① filter(quantity > 0) · ② withColumn('revenue', q * p) · ③ groupBy('country', 'month').agg(...) · ④ dense_rank().over(Window.partitionBy('country')). Which ones shuffle, and why?
① and ② are row-local — each row's outcome depends on that row alone, so every executor works in place; no shuffle. ③ shuffles: all rows of a country-month must meet in one partition to be summed. ④ also shuffles: ranking within a country requires the whole country in one place, sorted. The window keeps every row where groupBy collapses them — but both pay the same movement bill first.
The question "does this need rows to meet?" predicts every shuffle. If yes, plan for movement; if no, it is free parallelism.

5 · When NOT to use Spark

Spark is not automatically better — its costs are real

Setup cost

A JVM, a session, Java 17+, worker processes — seconds of overhead before the first row, and a heavier debugging surface when anything breaks.

Serialization cost

Data crossing the Python/JVM boundary gets encoded and decoded. pandas touches memory directly; Spark pays a toll at the border.

Shuffle cost

Every groupBy, join, and window may move rows between workers. At laptop scale this is pure overhead over a pandas groupby.

The symmetric mistake

Using pandas on data that does not fit is a crash; using Spark on data that fits easily is a slow, complicated version of a one-liner. Both are engine-choice errors.

The honest heuristic

If the data fits comfortably in memory and one machine finishes in acceptable time — the smaller engine wins. Reach up only when a real limit is in sight.

Use Spark when the data or the workload justifies its costs — and be able to say which cost the scale is buying you out of.
5 · Checkpoint

The 300 MB question

A colleague hands you a 300 MB CSV of transactions and says "let's do this properly — I'll spin up Spark." Your laptop has 16 GB of RAM. What do you say?
Politely: no. 300 MB fits in memory roughly fifty times over — pd.read_csv loads it in seconds and every Lesson 2–9 tool applies immediately. Spark would add JVM startup, Python↔JVM serialization, and shuffle machinery to a problem with none of the pain those exist to solve. If durability or constraints matter, SQLite is the step up — not a cluster. Spark enters when data stops fitting or one machine stops finishing: tens of gigabytes and beyond, or work that genuinely wants many machines.
"Properly" means matched to the problem. The burden of proof is always on the bigger engine.
5 · The through-line

Lesson 4 asked "what happens when n doubles?" — this lesson answers at the far end

Know the shapeLesson 4: cost grows with a shape. Distributed or not, a linear scan is a linear scan — Spark changes the constant, never the shape.
Don't do the workPartition pruning and pushed-down filters skip data entirely — the same win as choosing a better algorithm, achieved by layout.
Name the bottleneckAt cluster scale the expensive resource is movement (shuffles), not arithmetic. Optimize what dominates.
Then parallelizePartitions divide the constant by the worker count — powerful, but the last resort after shape, pruning, and movement are right.
Scaling is a ladder: better shape, less data touched, less data moved, more machines — in that order.
